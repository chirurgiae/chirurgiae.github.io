Emil Theodor Kocher (1841 - 1917)

Dr. Theodor Kocher, Prof. of Surgery
and Director of the Surgical clinic,
University of Bern

Swiss physician and medical researcher who received the 1909 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for his work in the physiology, pathology and surgery of the thyroid. The first Swiss citizen and the first surgeon to ever receive a Nobel prize, he was considered a pioneer and leader in the field of surgery in his time.

Among his many accomplishments are the introduction and promotion of aseptic surgery and scientific methods in surgery, specifically reducing the mortality of thyroidectomies below 1% in his operations.

Kocher’s extensive correspondence with the leading American surgeons of that time, William Halsted and Harvey Cushing describes his numerous experiments in the various fields of surgery : blood-saving methods, surgical treatment of epilepsy, antisepsis, aspiration prophylaxis, chloroform narcosis, regional anesthesia, the use of intravenous salt solutions. Other significant contributions include his works on techniques of reduction of shoulder dislocation [Kocher's Method], surgical infectious diseases, gunshot wounds, acute osteomyelitis, the theory of strangulated hernia, and abdominal surgery.

A number of instruments including a craniometer, a chloroform mask, an ether mask, artery and bowel clamps, scissors, chisels, files, wound-hooks, Kocher’s hemostatic forceps and Kocher’s dissector are credited to him. Furthermore procedures like Kocher manoeuvre, incisions - (R) subcostal and the thyroid incision, as well as Kocher-Debre-Semelaigne syndrome are named after him.

For his scientific attitude to practical surgery and the ability to solve the medical problems that came his way, he shall forever be remembered as a surgical maestro.
