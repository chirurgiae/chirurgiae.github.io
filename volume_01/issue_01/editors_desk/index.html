Editor's Desk

Publish or Perish

Despite the ocean of clinical wealth that we come across in our wards daily, most of us overlook the immense potential these hold and fail to take the effort to share this with the rest of the academic world. To change this unfortunate scenario, inculcating the publishing culture among our clinical class is vital. It is with this aspiration that we bring our humble work before you. We hope that this will help and encourage our young surgeons to take their first step towards academic publishing. This editorial is an effort to explain its importance in the current academic setting.

"when you orient your scholarship towards its obvious yet overlooked purpose - furthering human knowledge - its value does not need to be determined by others, because the value lies in the work itself"

First, who would not get excited at the prospect of seeing our name on the cover of a beautiful, glossy book or the credits of a high impact journal?

Beyond this obvious sense of self satisfaction and personal pleasure of sharing and writing about your work, there are many merits to publishing your work. The most apparent advantage is the additional line it provides on your CV - a very attractive one especially to the eyes of prospective employers. Academics on the hiring panel, particularly of teaching hospitals will cast a critical eye over the applicantâ€™s list of current and pending publications, carefully considering the scholarly value you would bring to the department.

Another reason for publishing in academia is to get your name out in the field and build your academic reputation. Simply put, advertisement. Also interactions with other researchers will only further your cause and may pave the way for some collaborative work.

One of the oft cited reasons for steering clear of publishing is the fear of rejection. But the constructive criticism and comments from reviewers itself help improve your academic work and extend your knowledge in the field. We forget that even the most established scholars face rejection. Most importantly, in-depth, clearly written scholarly research has its own value: it can reshape understanding, inform policy, and even help save lives, assuming the work is accessible. Lastly, when you orient your scholarship towards its obvious yet overlooked purpose - furthering human knowledge - its value does not need to be determined by others, because the value lies in the work itself. But papers, articles and journals, unfortunately are often sidelined amidst the humdrum of our daily life. Let this not be the case.

Publishing in academia is an important career step; but, like all things that are worthwhile, it takes time; it also gets easier with practice.

So wait no more...

Start publishing...

Assistant Professor
Dept. of General Surgery
Medical College, Thiruvananthapuram
