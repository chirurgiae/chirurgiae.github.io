chirurgia
chi·rur·gia (noun combining form) \ˌkīˈrərjēə, -ˈrə̄j-, -ˈrəij-\
surgery;cutting. Etymology from chīrurgus (“surgeon”)
MBBS
Medicinae Baccalaureus, Baccalaureus Chirurgiae
[Bachelor of Medicine, Bachelor of Surgery]
MCh
Magister Chirurgiae [Master of Surgery]

The dictionary defines surgery as "a branch of medicine concerned with diseases and conditions requiring or amenable to operative, manual or instrumental procedures". The root of the word can be traced to ancient Greece, where it was a combination of words that meant "hand work". Surgery is often likened to 'Art'; both involve acquiring 'skill as a result of learning or practice'.

"Ancora imparo" "I am still learning", the 88-year-old Michelangelo scribbled on the margin of a sketchbook as he was working on his last unfinished masterpiece, highlighting that the pursuit of knowledge is a lifelong process. Comparably, it takes nothing short of a lifetime of wholehearted dedication and self-discipline to carry oneself to the heights of the mastery of the art of Surgery. The surgeon must thus continually improve their execution while keeping pace with unremitting scientific and technological developments.

To quote Hippocrates, the Ancient Greek physician,

"Vita brevis,
ars longa,
occasio praeceps,
experimentum periculosum,
iudicium difficile"
"Life is short,
and art long,
opportunity fleeting,
experience perilous,
and decision difficult"
