Spontaneous Hemoperitoneum in Hepatocellular Carcinoma: A Dramatic Presentation

Dr. Mohanraj. M, Dr. Anil Kumar.S, Dr. Vijayakumaran Pillai, Dr. Rajmohan, Dr. Anilkumar.A.V

Dept. of Surgery, Medical College Hospital, Thiruvananthapuram

Abstract

Non-traumatic hemoperitoneum is rare and is often a result of gynecological disorders. This is a case of spontaneous hemoperitoneum following rupture of a hepatocellular carcinoma. Patient presented in shock and after stabilizing the vitals, a laparotomy was done that revealed a cavitating lesion in the liver segment 6 that was packed and later re-explored, packs removed and closed after achieving hemostasis. We would like to highlight the need to tailor the management of ruptured HCC according to individual presentations and stress that there is still a role for surgical exploration and conservative management compared to transcatheter arterial chemoembolization or immediate hepatectomy.

Introduction

Described below is a dramatic presentation of non-traumatic hemoperitoneum due to the rupture of a hepatocellular carcinoma. Only 6% of HCCs present as a spontaneous hemoperitoneum but very rarely as a gross hemoperitoneum with the patient in shock [1]. The patient was managed in the conventional manner of surgical packing and re-exploration rather than the gold standard transcatheter arterial chemoembolization [TACE] but still with comparable success, also avoiding the complications of TACE like hepatic infarction, abscess, gastroduodenal ulceration and lipiodol embolization[2].

Case Details

A 35 yr old female presented in shock with a distended abdomen and a history of abdominal pain for 4 hrs and breathlessness. She had a history of amennorhea for 2 months and no OCP intake. Despite intensive resuscitation 1 hr, her vitals were still unstable with no significant urine output. An abdominal sonogram revealed gross hemoperitoneum and a hyperechoic lesion in the liver prompting a laparotomy. Per-op findings showed 2.5 litres of hemoperitoneum and a necrotic tissue floating in the peritoneal cavity. Ovary, tubes and uterus were normal. A cavitating lesion was found in segment 6 of the liver with active bleeding which could not be controlled with electrocoagulation.Hence the right paracolic gutter was packed, gelfoam pieces placed over the cavity and abdomen closed. Patientâ€™s clinical status did not improve for 48 hrs and embolization could not be undertaken due to the unstable vitals and relative contraindications like a cirrhotic liver with ascites.

Abdomen was reopened after 80 hrs when the patient was relatively stable but had started developing initial signs of sepsis. Packs were removed and very minimal ooze was found from the cavity and cavity was covered with gelfoam pieces. Patient improved clinically, drain amounts slowly decreased to minimal and was removed, the patient discharged on Post-op day 20. Histopathology of the necrotic tissue showed moderately differentiated HCC with serum AFP values of 1318 ng/mL [normal - 0-8.5 ng/mL]

Discussion

Only 6% of cases of HCC present as hemoperitoneum and only < 1% present as a state of shock [3]. The diagnosis is usually established with CECT abdomen in a stable patient. Treatment is TACE with selective embolization of the supplying vessel [4] which gained popularity after the 1990s. Earlier approach was conservative but morality was high [>90%]. But in an unstable patient TACE may be difficult and hence a combination of surgical packing with TACE and re-exploration may be the treatment of choice. Thus the treatment of ruptured HCC should be tailored to the individual case.
