Adult intussusception as a rare cause of intestinal obstruction

Dr. Lakshmi P L, Dr. Sreekumar. A, Dr. Thomas. P.K, Dr. Sulfekar. M.S, Dr. Meer Chisthi, Dr. Rajendran. M.C, Dr. Gasnafar Hussain

Dept. of Surgery, Medical College Hospital, Thiruvananthapuram

Abstract

Intussusception is a rare cause of intestinal obstruction in adults [1 to 5 percent of mechanical bowel obstructions]. Described here is a 55 year old perimenopausal female who had no history of abdominal surgeries other than sterilization, presenting with recurrent symptoms of intestinal obstruction. Subsequently a diagnostic laparoscopy with adhesiolysis was done outside only to have symptoms recur yet again. Further imaging revealed intussusception of the descending colon into the sigmoid. A colonoscopic biopsy confirmed an adenocarcinoma of the splenic flexure acting as the lead point. A left hemicolectomy was done and the post-op period was unevnetful. The patient is now on follow-up.

Introduction

Intestinal intussusception is rare in adults, accounting for 1 to 5 percent of mechanical bowel obstructions [1,2] and less than 5% of the total cases of intussusceptions[3]. It often poses clinicians with a diagnostic conundrum owing to its varying presentations.

By definition it is the telescoping or invagination of one segment of gut into another. This is predominantly in a proximal to distal direction, that of the natural physiological peristaltic process.

The aetiology has multiple dimensions to its description. These include its location in the gastrointestinal tract, presence or absence of a physical or functional initiating lesion (the lead point) and the behavioural characteristics of this lead point, be it benign or malignant. The manifestations of intussusception fall anywhere between an acute and chronic intestinal obstruction. Etiologically it can be classified into primary or secondary type. Intussusceptions with no clear lead point are the primary or idiopathic type, present in 8%–20% of cases, and more likely to occur in the small intestine [4,5]. The secondary type, constituting the greater majority, is due to an existing gut pathological lesion - a lead point: a functional or structural lesion associated with the intussusceptum, postulated to be the ‘trigger’ for intussusception. The commonest ‘lead points’ are colonic malignant tumours, [60%]. Benign tumours constitute the majority of the remaining 40%.

Case Details

A 55 year old perimenopausal female, with no comorbidities and without any past abdominal surgeries other than a post-partum sterilization 18 yrs back, presented with left sided colicky abdominal pain associated with multiple episodes bilious vomiting and constipation for 2 days. She had simililar episodes in the past which responded to conservative management. There was no history of weight loss/ loss of appetite/malena/ hematochezia/ abdominal distention. As she was having recurrent episodes, she underwent diagnostic laproscopy with adhesiolysis from a private hospital. Pain recurred along with constipation and vomiting after one month of the procedure. On examination, vitals were stable . Abdominal examination revealed a distended abdomen with a vague non tender mass in left lumbar region about 5x5 cm. Bowel sounds were absent. Rectal examination showed no ballooning or active bleeding, or any mass palpable.

Erect abdominal radiograph showed dilated bowel loops that prompted a CECT abdomen, which showed typical target sign - the bowel in bowel appearance in the left iliac fossa; part of descending colon invaginating into sigmoid colon with wall thickening. Colonoscopy showed globulated swelling at splenic flexure 6x6 cm. Biopsy report was that of a moderately differentiated adeno carcinoma. Except for mild elevation of CEA - 4.41 [0-3.4 ng/ml], the rest of her investigations were normal. We went ahead with laprotomy that disclosed an annular constricting growth in lower part of descending colon telescoping into the sigmiod. Proximal descending colon and transverse colon were dilated and there were extensive omental adhesions to the parietal wall. Liver and the rest of viscera were normal and there was no ascites. Left hemicolectomy with an end to end anastomosis of transverse colon to the rectum was done. Post-op period was uneventful and she was discharged.

Histopathology showed a signet cell carcinoma of the colon measuring 9x 5x 2.8 cm involving the entire thickness of the wall infiltrating the serosa. Adjacent mesocolon showed secondary deposits. Resected margins were tumor free. 7/7 lymph nodes were positive. Also perineural and lymphatic invasion were noted.

Discussion

The first report of intussusception was made in 1674 by Barbette of Amsterdam. Intussusception or "introsusception" as it was called then, was in addition detailed in 1789 by John Hunter[6]. In 1871 Sir Jonathan Hutchinson was the first to successfully operate on a child with intussusceptions[7].

Adult intussusception occurs infrequently and differs from the childhood condition in its presentation, aetiology, and treatment.

The common intussusceptions have been classified into four categories according to the site of origin: enteric, ileocolic, ileocaecal, and colonic. Enteric and colonic cases are those that are confined to the small and large intestine respectively. While ileocolic intussusceptions are those with prolapse of the ileum into the colon through the ileocaecal valve, ileocaecal intussusceptions occur when the ileocaecal valve acts as the lead point.

However, in clinical practice it is difficult to differentiate between ileocolic and ileocaecal types. The presenting symptoms in adults are non-specific and often long standing. Most series report pain as the commonest symptom, [71% to 90%] followed by vomiting and bleeding from the rectum. Abdominal mass is noted in 24% to 42% of cases.

Diagnosis can be delayed because of its longstanding, intermittent, and non-specific symptoms and most cases are diagnosed at an emergency laparotomy. It is thus important to have a high index of suspicion. With more frequent use of computed tomography in the evaluation of patients with abdominal pain, the condition can be diagnosed more reliably.

Treatment requires resection of the involved bowel without attempted reduction in colonic lesions or in small bowel cases where the bowel is nonviable or where malignancy is suspected. With gastroduodenal and coloanal intussusceptions the surgical technique may need modification according to the situation and can be challenging.
