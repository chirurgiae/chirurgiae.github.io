Metastatic malignant phyllodes tumour: An interesting presentation

Dr. Sivaram Premnath, Dr. Jayan Chandrasekharan, Dr. Joby John, Dr. Krishna Kumar, Dr. Sunil Sundaram, Dr. Rahul Muraleedharan, Dr. Rakhul.L.R

Dept. of Surgery, Medical College Hospital, Thiruvananthapuram

Abstract

The phyllodes tumour is a rare breast tumour [< 1% of all breast tumours]. In this case, a patient diagnosed with metastatic malignant Phyllodes Tumour with an unusual presentation is described. A 55 year old lady with a history of right simple mastectomy for benign phyllodes, presented with a slow growing left parotid region swelling. Further workup demonstrated lytic lesions on the sacrum, L5 vertebrae and head and neck of right femur. Detailed imaging of the first swelling revealed the lesion to be a 'Heterogeneous lytic lesion involving ramus of left mandible, with extension into masticator space'. A metastatic malignant phyllodes was diagnosed and she was offered palliative therapy. Only a few cases of osseus phyllodes metastasis esp. to the mandible are reported.

Introduction

The phyllodes tumour is a rare breast tumour, accounting for less than 1% of all breast neoplasms[1]. They present as a breast lump at any age including adolescence.The peak incidence in women is between 35 and 55 years of age, with only a few cases reported in men[3]. A large series from the M.D. Anderson Cancer Centre reported the incidence of phyllodes tumours histologically as benign (58%), intermediate (12%), and malignant (30%)[4]. Wide local excision is usually the primary approach to treatment. Reoccurrence rate after surgical excision is variously described as between 14% and 21% with recurrence more likely in those with the tumour at the margins of excision[5]. Malignant phyllodes tumours behave like sarcomas and can develop blood-borne metastases with a poor prognosis[6]. Here, a patient diagnosed with metastatic malignant Phyllodes Tumour with an unusual presentation is described. The aim includes adding information to the relatively small database of malignant phyllodes tumour cases reported.

Case Report

A 55 year old lady presented to the OPD with complaints of a swelling in left parotid region. She first noticed the swelling about 7 months back and it was gradually increasing in size. The swelling was initially painful, but later turned painless. There was no increase in size of swelling with meals/difficulty in opening of the mouth/difficulty in speech, alteration of voice or deviation of the angle of the mouth. She had a history of simple mastectomy for right breast 1 and 1/2 years back but details of were not available initially. She also had a past history of total abdominal hysterectomy 6 years back for fibroids. She was on regular analgesics from a local hospital for hip pain.

General examination was unremarkable but for the antalgic gait due to the hip pain. On local examination there was a swelling of size 5x4 cm below and slightly in front of lobule of left ear, which was raising the ear lobule. It was bony-hard in consistency with a smooth surface and well defined borders. There were no features of facial nerve involvement /restriction of the movements of the jaw/ cervical lymph node enlargement. Systemic examination revealed the healed scars of previous surgeries, a Pfannenstiel incision scar over lower abdomen and a right mastectomy scar. Hip joint movements were restricted by pain. Localized tenderness was present over the lumbosacral spine. With the above clinical scenario we reached the clinical diagnosis of a parotid tumour with a suspicion of malignancy.

Upon further investigating the patient, the routine preoperative investigations were normal. Patient already had an FNAC of the swelling from an outside lab giving a report suggestive of a pleomorphic adenoma. The strong clinical suspicion of malignancy made us repeat the FNAC; yielding 'Cytology findings of a benign neoplasm: Pleomorphic Adenoma to be considered'. We ordered a USG of the left parotid region, which was again reported as a pleomorphic adenoma.The patient was complaining of severe hip pain, for which we ordered an X-ray of the pelvic region that showed multiple lytic lesions on the sacrum, L5 vertebrae and head and neck of right femur (fig.1,2). The FNAC and USG reports seemed contradictory to our clinical findings and we took a CT scan of head and neck region, that revealed a 'Heterogeneous lytic lesion involving ramus of left mandible, with extension into masticator space. Left parotid gland normal and displaced posteriorly. A lytic lesion is seen in left scapula'(fig. 3). An USG guided FNAC was done and the report came as ‘suggestive of a metastasis from a spindle cell neoplasm’. An USG Abdomen showed two small hypo-echoic lesions in the segment V of liver, possibly metastasis. In the meantime we were able to collect the details of the previous simple mastectomy that was done at the Regional Cancer Centre, Thiruvananthapuram; the review of histopathology slides was reported as Phyllodes tumour / Spindle Cell Carcinoma. We reached a final diagnosis of metastasis from a malignant phyllodes tumour. At present she is undergoing palliative treatment from our oncology department.

Discussion

Phyllodes tumours are rare fibro-epithelial lesions that account for less than 1% of all breast neoplasms [9, 10]. Johannes Müller in 1838[11], coined the term Cystosarcoma phyllodes; a misnomer as the tumours are rarely cystic and the majority follow a benign clinical course. In case of clinical suspicion, the first step in workup is a mammogram which shows a hyperdense, large, rounded or lobulated mass with distinct margins[12]. Further, breast ultrasound is the imaging modality of choice to characterize a breast mass and a suspected phyllodes tumour. Sonographically, not only is it difficult to differentiate phyllodes tumour from a fibroadenoma, but the ability to grade it is also limited[13]. The role of CT is usually to assess for the degree of local invasion, recurrence or for the presence of metastasis[13]. Currently, there are few reports describing the imaging appearance of osseous phyllodes tumour metastases. While metastasis can present as a solid mass adjacent to the involved bone, it appears to rapidly infiltrate the cortex and medulla resulting in a permeative pattern on radiographs. Bone scan may show increased Tc99m-MDP radiotracer uptake. MRI may show a diffusely infiltrated bone with complete marrow replacement, avid enhancement, cortical destruction and significant surrounding soft tissue edema and may better delineate the extent of metastasis into the surrounding tissues.

The phyllodes tumour is classified based on histological grade as benign, borderline or malignant. Grading is by evaluating the frequency of mitoses, the characteristics of the tumour margins, the stromal cellularity and the degree of atypia[14]. Another histological finding which is worrisome for increased aggressiveness is stromal overgrowth, defined as the presence of stroma without associated epithelial elements over one low power (x 40) field[12].

With increasing tumour grade, the risk of metastasis also increase. Distant metastases develops in 10% overall[15] and 20% of histologically malignant tumours. Most distant metastases develop without evidence of local recurrence[16]. The commonest sites for distant metastases are the lung, bone, and abdominal viscera[17, 18]. These often occur in the absence of lymph node metastases and histologically contain only the stromal element. The risk of metastatic disease is not influenced by the extent of the initial surgery and appears to be predetermined by tumour biology. Few reports have been published of distant metastases after excision of a benign phyllodes tumour, except when histologically malignant local recurrence occurs.

Regardless of grade, the primary method of treatment is surgical - local excision, wide local excision or mastectomy. Wide excision is recommended because of the strong tendency for local recurrence (upto 20%), even in benign phyllodes tumours. Secondary treatments include adjuvant radiotherapy and/or chemotherapy. As they undergo haematogenous spread, the proportion of patients with lymph node metastases is approximately 10% and routine axillary clearance is not recommended. Metastatic phyllodes tumours have a poor prognosis and no long term survival studies has been reported. Suzuki-Uematsu et al. reviewed 15 cases of malignant PTs and described a five year survival rate following primary surgery of 10% while the 2.2 year survival rate following detection of metastasis was 11%. Isolated reports have been published of good palliation of metastatic disease from single agent or combination chemotherapy[19, 20] but the exact role of chemotherapy in metastatic phyllodes tumours remains to be defined.
